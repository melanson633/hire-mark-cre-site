NEW ENGLAND CONSTRUCTION QUARTERLY 03 / 11
PATTERN I · MASSACHUSETTS

The approvals collapse became visible.

Boston's planning board approved $4.8 billion of development in 2025, down roughly half from the prior year and the lowest total in a decade. Statewide, Boston Indicators reported Massachusetts building permits down 44% from the 2021 peak.

The pipeline entering Q1 was already thin. The quarter did not produce a reversal.
WHY IT MATTERS

Massachusetts-only builders should plan for 2026 volume below 2024, even in favored sectors. Financing costs did not reset, and policy layers are now part of the underwriting stack.

SOURCEBoston Globe, WBUR, Boston Indicators, Banker & Tradesman

PATTERN I · NEW HAMPSHIRE

The permits boom moved north.

New Hampshire municipalities issued permits for 5,822 housing units in 2025, the highest annual total in two decades. The state has added roughly 25,688 units since 2020, with growth concentrated in the Southern Tier and Seacoast.

Twenty-three towns and cities accounted for 63% of permits while representing 43.5% of population.
WHY IT MATTERS

This is present reallocation, not a future trend. GCs, A/E firms, and developers that can work across the MA-NH border are absorbing demand that once stayed south.

SOURCENHPR, Manchester Ink Link, Concord Monitor, Banker & Tradesman

PATTERN II · TARIFFS

Repricing became the base case.

Nonresidential construction input prices rose at a 12.6% annualized rate in the first two months of 2026, per ENR's 1Q cost report. Steel, aluminum, and copper products carry 50% tariffs; derivatives carry 25%; softwood lumber sits at 10% with various derivatives at 25%.

AGC found 70% of contractors affected by actual or proposed tariffs; 40% raised bid prices.
WHY IT MATTERS

The February Supreme Court decision did not remove the main construction exposure. Escalation clauses, indexed resets, and early procurement on steel-heavy scopes are now table stakes.

SOURCEENR, AGC Tariff Resource Center, Utility Dive, AGC & Sage

PATTERN III · LIFE SCIENCES

The lab market bifurcated.

Greater Boston life-sciences vacancy closed 2025 near 28%, with roughly 17M SF available. Cambridge held steadier at about 22%; suburban inventory hit 30.5%. In Somerville, 74 Middlesex Avenue remains entirely vacant after delivering 465,000 SF in early 2025.

Roche tripled its Harvard ERC commitment to 100,000 SF while speculative inventory sat.
WHY IT MATTERS

Lab work is clustering around anchor-let campuses and conversion projects. Net-new speculative lab is no longer the default growth market for construction teams.

SOURCECambridge Day, Harvard Crimson, Bloomberg, Boston.gov

INFRASTRUCTURE · OFFSHORE WIND

Vineyard Wind reached completion.

On March 13, crews installed the final blades on Vineyard Wind 1's sixty-second turbine. The 800 MW project, fifteen miles south of Martha's Vineyard, became the first large-scale offshore wind project completed in the United States after a Q1 legal fight over a federal stop-work order.

Sixty-two GE Haliade-X turbines and 186 blades are now in place.
WHY IT MATTERS

For New England's energy and construction economies, the project proves a large offshore wind build can physically finish in the current federal environment. It does not prove the next project gets the same path.

SOURCEWBUR, Vineyard Gazette, Martha's Vineyard Times, NWF

NEW ENGLAND CONSTRUCTION QUARTERLY 08 / 11
INSTITUTIONAL · HEALTHCARE

Healthcare entered a megacycle.

Dana-Farber and BIDMC began site-preparation deconstruction on a roughly $1.6B inpatient cancer hospital at One Joslin Place. In the same quarter, Mass General Brigham accepted a $35M gift toward the $1.9B, 2M SF Ragon Building on its Cambridge Street campus.

Longwood and Cambridge Street are institutional-owner construction pipelines running through the end of the decade.
WHY IT MATTERS

Healthcare is one of the few sectors where large owners are committing fresh capital into a high-rate environment. For hospital-capable GCs, pipeline visibility is unusually long.

SOURCEDana-Farber / BIDMC, Boston Globe, MGH Giving
