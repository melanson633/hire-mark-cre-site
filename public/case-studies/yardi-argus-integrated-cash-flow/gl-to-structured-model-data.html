Yardi-Argus Model Bridge: Turning GL Actuals and Argus Forecasts Into One Asset-Management Model

Built a reusable Excel and Power Query architecture that transformed Yardi GL exports and Argus cash-flow forecasts into a single monthly actual-to-forecast model for CRE asset management. The system improved short-to-medium term distribution forecasting, supported longer-term IRR time-series analysis, and let asset managers drill into structured GL detail without waiting for routine accounting pulls.

Scope Clarity - Three Related Use Cases

1. Yardi GL-only transformation.Raw GL exports became a normalized pivot-table output for structured actuals review. This pattern could be used across 70+ portfolio properties as needed.
2. Yardi + Argus integrated model.Historical actuals, Argus forecasts, debt, cash, and net working capital logic landed in one drill-down workbook.
3. Investor-facing summary model.Quarterly reporting used summary Excel outputs, not the full GL-detail drill-down, because the detailed workbook was too sensitive to share externally.

Net working capital means short-term balance-sheet accounts such as receivables, payables, prepaids, and accruals that affect actual cash available for distributions or capital calls.

Model Output - Actuals To Forecast In One View

The working model compressed historical accounting detail and forward Argus forecasts into a model-readable monthly cash-flow view. The red dotted line in the screenshot separates Yardi GL actual results on the left from future Argus forecast periods on the right.

Why it mattered: a single row structure made monthly spikes, trend changes, distribution timing, and return sensitivity easier to inspect without forcing asset managers back into disconnected accounting and forecast files.

Drill-Down Capability - Accounting Detail Without Routine Pulls

The model retained transaction-level support behind the summary rows. Reviewers could move from a monthly NOI or capex variance to the underlying rows and focus first on the largest cash-flow drivers.

System Architecture - Yardi, Argus, Mapping, Model

The architecture decision was the hard part: keep accounting actuals, forecast rows, debt, cash, mapping tables, and working-capital logic separate at the source, then normalize them into one consolidated dataset. That made the model reusable across properties and reduced the rebuild burden for new acquisitions.

Transformation Logic

Power Query handled after setup

  • Folder-file ingestion made the update pattern simple: export the GL report, place it in the property folder, refresh.
  • Header cleanup, column selection, debit-credit sign normalization, account mapping, and appended actual/forecast/debt rows ran through the query layer.
  • Model outputs then fed pivot-table review, while cash distributions, contribution/distribution tracking, and other KPIs were tracked natively in Excel using OFFSET / INDEX-MATCH style formulas outside the pivot.
  • A new GL-period and Argus-forecast refresh could take 2-3 minutes once the model was configured.

Controls And Refresh Process

The workflow was not a black box. Outputs were reviewed before internal decisions or external summaries. The strongest controls were cash tie-outs, T6 NOI checks, visible-period review, and variance troubleshooting driven by the transaction-level architecture.

Cash tie-out.Cash in the model was manually compared to Yardi for the latest reporting period before the model was used.
T6 NOI tie-out.Trailing-six NOI was checked against the latest reporting period to confirm the transformed actuals were behaving correctly.
Refresh sequencing.Some models required refresh-all twice: once for queries and once for the pivot/data model. New period filters were reviewed if months did not immediately appear.
Human review.Outputs were reviewed internally, then summarized for external JV reporting. Full drill-down workbooks were not shared externally because of GL-detail sensitivity.

Cash, Net Working Capital, Distribution Logic

DistributionCapacity = MAX(0, CurrentCash + PriorPeriodCashFlow - MinimumReserve - NearTermCapexAndLeasingCosts - ScheduledDebtService - NetWorkingCapitalBurnoff)
Pseudocode: start with current cash, add prior-period cash flow, hold back the minimum reserve, subtract near-term capital costs, scheduled debt service, and expected net working capital burn-off, then treat any positive residual as potential distribution capacity. In one Q4 2025 decision-support example, this balance-sheet-grounded view supported roughly $800K lower JV partner fund-level capital calls versus forward-only models.

Known Improvement Areas

The model was intentionally kept in use because the existing process was producing high-quality results, but the architecture had clear improvement areas. The original build reflected the tooling, timeline, and operating requirements at the time: refresh behavior could be cleaner, period filters could be more automated, and a modern rebuild could use a more formal data layer and AI-assisted documentation/testing around the same finance logic.

Qualitative Impact

  • Faster portfolio visibility: recurring quarterly investor reporting started from a fuller actual-to-forecast model.
  • Better variance review: side-by-side presentation and drill-down made spikes easier to spot and investigate.
  • Stronger forecast confidence: IRR projections considered current cash, debt, and net working capital timing instead of relying on forward models alone.
  • Lower accounting dependency: after update, standard accounting pulls were generally unnecessary unless the model surfaced a specific outlier.

Transferable Relevance

  • Finance systems judgment: chose Excel and Power Query because they matched the users, source systems, and review workflow.
  • Accounting-to-FP&A bridge: mapped GL accounts, Argus forecast rows, cash, debt, net working capital, and IRR logic into one usable model.
  • Automation readiness: the same pattern applies anywhere standard GL exports must become structured decision data.
  • AI-workflow relevance: because this was built pre-AI, it shows the underlying process architecture that can now be improved faster with current tools.
