Before / After

BeforeAfter
Lease abstracts were often missing for internal portfolio signings, leaving key deal terms trapped between Asset Management, Property Management, and Accounting. During acquisitions, outside counsel abstracts were expensive, slow, minimally traceable, and not tuned to RJ Kelly's underwriting metrics. Leases could be processed into a consistent abstract, workbook, and JSON structure. Rent terms and schedules became easier to compare against seller-provided Argus forecast models, and key language could be skimmed with citations back to source documents.

What Mark Built

Agent skill architecture

Built and modularized the workflow for Shortcut.ai, Claude Code, Claude Cowork, Codex, and Gemini-style agents while preserving a consistent output structure.

Rich schema layer

Designed structured JSON outputs for future lease databasing rather than stopping at a one-off narrative abstract.

Review controls

Included source citations, validation status, open items, change logs, and a human-in-the-loop checkpoint before final output generation.

Business-specific parsing

Focused the abstraction around internal underwriting, PM, accounting, and executive review needs instead of generic attorney abstract formatting.

Business Impact

Impact AreaConfirmed effect
Underwriting Lease economics and key terms became easier to compare against Argus forecast models, shortening the path from document intake to underwriting feedback.
Internal handoff New lease signings gained a clearer bridge from Asset Management to Property Management and Accounting, reducing buried lease knowledge.
Review quality Internal users qualitatively preferred the company-aligned outputs over third-party attorney abstracts because they were more traceable and easier to skim for operating decisions.
Transferability The same pattern translated messy contract review into a controlled AI workflow with schema design, agent routing, citations, Excel integration, and reusable outputs.
